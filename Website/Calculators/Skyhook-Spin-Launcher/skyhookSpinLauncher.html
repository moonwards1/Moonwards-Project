Skyhook + Tip Spin-Launcher Calculator

A gravity-gradient skyhook is a long vertical tether whose centre of mass orbits a body at orbital speed. The tether does not rotate — it stays aligned with the local vertical, held taut by the gravity gradient (the tidal force). Because the whole structure shares the centre of mass's angular velocity, the top travels faster than the local circular-orbit speed, so there centrifugal effect exceeds gravity and it is flung outward, while the foot travels slower than local orbital speed, so there gravity exceeds centrifugal effect and it is pulled inward. These opposing pulls put the tether in tension, greatest at the centre of mass. The slow, low-sweeping foot is ideal for pick-up; the fast-moving top gives a payload a boost on release.

The centre of mass is where gravity and centrifugal effect balance — the tension maximum, and the top of the effective-potential hill, analogous to the L1 point of a Moon–L1 elevator. Climbing foot → centre is uphill; centre → top is downhill (regenerative).

Why station-keeping propellant scales with angular momentum, not Δv

A natural first guess is to take the cargo's change in speed from foot to top, multiply by its mass, and call that the momentum the engines must replace. That under-counts, because in orbit the conserved currency is angular momentum (mass × speed × radius), not linear momentum — gravity points straight at the planet's centre, so it exerts no torque and cannot change it. As cargo climbs, both its speed and its radius grow, so its angular momentum grows by more than the speed change alone. The figure shows the two effects, and how the thruster's mount radius (its lever arm) sets the final fuel bill. Numbers are for the default Moon setup (foot 10 km, centre 275 km, top 1000 km, exhaust 8 km/s).

The reconciliation in one line: j_real / j_Δv = (r_top + r_foot) / r_engine. Your spreadsheet captured Δv = ω(r_top − r_foot); the angular-momentum debt is ω(r_top² − r_foot²), and the difference factors as (r_top − r_foot)(r_top + r_foot). The extra (r_top + r_foot) is the radius growth you weren't counting; dividing by r_engine is the lever arm of where you push. For the default setup that factor is (2737 + 1747)/2012 = 2.23× at the centre of mass and (2737 + 1747)/2737 = 1.64× at the top — which is why a straight-line Δv guess of about 9.6% becomes 21.4% (CoM) or 15.7% (top).
