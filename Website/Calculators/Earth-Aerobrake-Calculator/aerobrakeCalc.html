Earth Aerobrake Reality-Check Calculator

Sizing check for the inbound Ceres-fleet manoeuvre in CeresTransportSystem.md: a ship arrives on a hyperbolic approach, makes a single grazing pass through Earth's upper atmosphere, and exits into a very eccentric orbit whose apoapsis sits near the edge of Earth's sphere of influence (where the inclination/periapsis burn for the Moon is cheap). The pass sheds only a small slice off the top of the entry speed, so this is the grazing regime, not a steep capsule entry: peak deceleration is set by the speed lost and the geometry, and is independent of ballistic coefficient — but the heating and dynamic pressure are not, and that is where the design is really tested. Pure orbital and entry maths come from OrbitalMath (../../Shared/math-utils.js).

How to read this

Peak g and the orbit are robust — they depend only on the speed shed and the geometry. Ballistic coefficient (set by mass ÷ Cd·area) is the swing variable: a compact, high-β ship must dive deep into dense air (huge dynamic pressure and heat flux), while a large, low-β decelerator brakes high and gently. Watch the periapsis altitude, dynamic pressure, and heat-flux readouts as you change the shield diameter.

Heat-shield mass is a convective-only estimate (Sutton–Graves flux × soak time ÷ heat of ablation, with a ×2.5 allowance for char, insulation and margin). It excludes radiative heating, which becomes significant above ~11 km/s and grows with nose radius — so the larger-diameter designs carry an unmodelled radiative penalty. Treat the TPS figure as a lower bound and a first-cut reality check, not a flight sizing. A proper orbital simulator plus a coupled radiative/ablation TPS code is needed to firm these up.
