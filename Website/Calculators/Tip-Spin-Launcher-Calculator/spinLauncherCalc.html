About a couple of the fields

Where the numbers come from (target periapsis): a plane change is an angle, but turning it into a velocity needs the speed of the vector being tilted — the ship's heliocentric in-plane speed at release. Release is exactly retrograde, so that speed is fixed by the target periapsis: the ship sits at the aphelion of its transfer ellipse (Ceres' distance), so v = √(GM☉(2/rCeres − 1/a)) with a = ½(rCeres + rperiapsis). That same speed sets the total retrograde throw needed (vCeres − v); the elevator supplies its tip speed and the spin launcher makes up the rest. So you only set the target periapsis and the plane-change angle — the retrograde boost and the velocity being tilted are derived. Out-of-plane component = v × tan(angle).

Axial tilt vs. required spin-plane tilt — two different angles: Axial tilt (4° for Ceres) is the fixed angle between Ceres' equator and its orbital plane. The elevator is anchored on the equator, so its tip sweeps the equatorial plane — already 4° off the orbital plane. The required spin-plane tilt reported above is a separate, adjustable angle: how far the spin launcher's own rotation plane must be tipped out of the retrograde-throw direction so that part of the tip velocity points out of plane and produces the plane change. It is just atan(out-of-plane ÷ retrograde) — the direction of the velocity the arm adds. Where that velocity has to point in inertial space (which is where axial tilt and orbital inclination come in) is a trajectory question for the other calculators; this tool only sizes the machine that delivers it.
