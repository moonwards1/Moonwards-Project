Mars-centred view. Mars is placed from its Keplerian heliocentric elements (Shared/orbit.js); Phobos is approximated as circular and exactly equatorial (its real ~1.5% eccentricity and ~1° inclination to Mars' equator are neglected), orbiting at its mean distance of 9,376 km. The skyhook is a rigid, gravity-gradient structure co-rotating with Phobos: since Phobos itself is the centre of mass, the whole tether — lower end, Phobos, upper end — traces concentric circles around Mars, not around Phobos. A point at radius r moves at ωr, where ω is Phobos' own orbital angular rate. Phobos has no meaningful sphere of influence (smaller than Phobos itself) and is treated as massless in the trajectory integration — only Mars and the Sun's gravity act on a released vessel. The released trajectory is integrated (RK4) under Mars + Sun gravity, with Mars at its real ephemeris position, so escape to a heliocentric orbit is continuous. Released "below Phobos" is sub-orbital by construction (the tether's fixed angular rate is slower than local circular speed at any radius under Phobos), so most such releases dive toward Mars; where the path crosses Mars' ~100 km atmosphere interface, the entry speed and flight-path angle are read from the integrated path, and the peak deceleration (Allen–Eggers) and peak convective heat flux (Sutton–Graves, using the classical Allen–Eggers peak-density/peak-speed point) are estimated for the given entry vehicle. This is a ballistic-entry estimate only — no descent, landing site or ground track is modelled. Up to two waypoint burns (prograde/radial/normal, km/s) can be added along the trajectory; each burn is resolved in the velocity frame of whichever body locally dominates at that point (Mars, or the Sun once well past Mars' SOI). Plane change is always measured against the ecliptic.
