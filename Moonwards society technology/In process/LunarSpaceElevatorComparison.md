# Architecture Comparison: Lunar Space Elevator vs. Gravity-gradient Lunar Skyhook / Mass Driver

- Safety factor to be used: 3

- Both installations use solar panels to collect energy, which is stored in flywheels, their rotors made of silica fiber as well

- Climbers for both installations store the energy needed to transit tethers in onboard flywheels, which they recharge when needed at waystations appropriately distributed along the tethers. In the case of climbers, to save rotor mass CNT fiber may be used.

- Climbers for both are currently designed with large wheels that have special, highly flexible rims, that pinch a narrow track between them. Tracks are made of magnesium alloy with diamond grit facings.

- The main purpose of both systems is delivery of cargo to Earth's surface

- They also have to facilitate operations on Ceres and Psyche at scale.

## Comparison

We need the pros and cons of an architecture that uses a space elevator, versus one that uses a skyhook and mass driver combination. The default values in the calculators for each system are set to the ones used for each architecture for the Earth moon system in this world-building project. The case used in the following table is Phase 2, for the skyhook system, when operations have expanded to Ceres and Psyche. Being able to expand in production volumes, interplanetary reach, and mission scope, in an efficient and robust way, is core to the aims of space development. If an architecture holds up well in an Earth-moon system context, but falls down when higher volumes or other celestial bodies are involved, it fails the test.

##### Note 1:

If the tether of the space elevator was extended to 222,000 km length, it could also release capsules directly into an atmospheric entry trajectory.  In that case, the amount of cargo on it at any time would be almost 400,000 tonnes, plus the mass of the climbers. The counterweight could be much smaller and provide enough tension to keep the whole structure stable. A calculation that shows what the tether mass would be with decent safety factor is needed, but I suspect it would have to be very heavy, and the corresponding increase of maintenance demands perhaps starts to get out of control. ## System expansions for Phase 2

To truly shine, the moon must expand its output. Also, exploration and development of other worlds requires transport infrastructure that frees the economics from the heavy demands of rocket engines - be that in huge fuel consumption, huge power consumption, huge thermal and/or radiation challenges, or very high engineering requirements. 

Phase 1 considers only what can be done with inputs the moon has itself, supplemented by a relatively modest amount of material from Earth. Phase 2 brings in Ceres and Psyche as the primary sources of many key chemicals the moon lacks, and that remain expensive to bring in from Earth at truly high volume.

There are three main options for fixed infrastructure to launch ships to Ceres. The final choice depends on a proper comparison of the full systems for each.

## Can the L1 space elevator compete in the Phase 2 paradigm

While the skyhook can be expanded to launch to Ceres and Psyche, the space elevator can only provide a modest Obert opportunity for such flights. A spin launcher to handle interplanetary launches would have to deal with a huge delta v boost, and require the counterweight become far larger.
