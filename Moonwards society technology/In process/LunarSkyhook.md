# Lunar Skyhook - Phase 1 (pre Ceres and Psyche operations)

## Parameters

- Orbits the moon's equator every 2.25 hours

- The top deck of the skyhook has a working estimate of 50 kilotonnes mass, made up of the items listed under the Top Deck section

- A budget of 20 kilotonnes is added to this to account for all the infrastructure along the upper tether. This provides a figure for calculation of the tension on the upper tether, that fudges in all the mass distributed along its length as a value added to the tension at the top, as the contribution to the total tension depends on the altitude of the mass. This mass includes:
  
  - the climber tracks
  
  - solar panel and energy storage installations, the tug fleet, and the fuel and servicing infrastructure for the tug fleet, at the main launch deck 
  
  - also electrical infrastructure, waystations with supplies and maintenance facilities, and repair and maintenance robots continuously working on the tether

## key locations along the skyhook include:

### the foot platform, from where shuttles return to the surface

- with a small load of cargo for the surface installations
- Altitude 10 km (lowered to 2 km when releasing shuttles returning to the ground, where they are captured by the catcher complex)

### The receiving deck, where shuttles arrive from the surface

- with payloads launched from the mass driver

- Altitude 233 km

- Shuttles arrive here moving just 1 m/s faster than the decks - the mass driver launch and small burns set that up

- In Phase 1, receives 80 to 90 shuttles per pass

### The center of mass, which is simply orbital altitude

- Altitude 275 km

- it is kept at a specific altitude through mass distribution along the tethers and orbital stationkeeping, but there isn't necessarily anything specific there

- Most mass has to be below this altitude in order to get the needed distribution. There is a very large habitat, and the industrial facilities, below it. 

### The outer docks, where ships arrive from off-world

- Altitude 606.5 km
- By first setting up an orbit of the moon at 6554 km altitude, ships can burn to set up approach for docking with a relative speed on arrival only 2 m/s faster than the docks

### The main launch deck, for traffic headed to Earth's surface or LEO

- Altitude 950 km
  - This is the altitude that spends the most time naturally set up to put a ship on a trajectory that provides good ballistic entry into Earth's atmosphere
- Full installation extends from 940 to 970 km altitude, within which length ships can be released with the added services of a push-off along the normal direction of up to 2 m/s, fuel chilling and power supply up to the moment of release, and no need to move onto a 'siding' to let climber traffic pass (as might be necessary for ships that do their own release from the tether above the launch deck)
- Depending on where the moon is in its orbit, and when the desired landing point on Earth will be in range, it can be best to launch from anywhere along the launch deck from the bottom to the top. The point chosen is often combined with a release a bit before or after the point that would be exactly retrograde. The release moment is mostly used to advance or delay atmospheric entry in order to have the landing point in range.
- The tug fleet waits here between jobs, and gets serviced and prepped

### Top deck, where station-keeping happens

- Altitude 2000 km
- It holds:
  - the array of VASIMR engines maintaining the skyhook's orbit
  - the solar panels providing them energy
  - their fuel tanks
  - the flywheels providing energy storage
  - repair and maintenance facilities
- Placing the boost engines at the very top of the structure gives them the highest torque, allowing them to return momentum to the system cheaply, in a manner very analogous to Oberth maneuvers
- They compensate for momentum lost as mass ascends the tethers (increases in altitude, though it experiences a net force pointing away from the moon once it passes the center of mass)

## Traffic

- Allowing 2% for down time, there are 3813 orbits per year where ships are received and launched as part of the skyhook's principal operations
  
  - Ships regularly arrive with cargo from Earth. This volume is minor in comparison with export volume, and is ignored for now.

- Releases 6 Earthdiver ships from the top every orbit, headed to Earth's surface, 
  
  - each massing 160 tonnes
  
  - Total mass released 960 tonnes.
  
  - Total cargo mass counts both the contents of the hold, and the ship itself, which is made of superior quality lunar alloys, ceramics, cermets, MMCs, and CNT cloth, designed to be broken up into easily resold pieces that hold their value. Only the heat shield is waste mass
  
  - 145.5 tonnes of saleable cargo per ship
  
  - Annual total 3.6 megatonnes

- Receives 88 shuttles every orbit,  each with 12 tonnes of cargo, delivering inputs for final manufacturing at the skyhook industrial zone, plus all fuel and supplies required for skyhook operations

## Phase 2 - expansion to Ceres and Psyche

Mining of Ceres at scale allows the moon to expand production of its most valuable bulk product: CNT cable and cloth. Many other crucial elements can be provided by Ceres if infrastructure is sufficiently capable: hydrogen, lithium, boron, nitrogen, fluorine, chlorine, and copper. The operation has to be at truly industrial scale to justify the investment. Ceres operations ramp up to launching 9 to 10 megatonnes of cargo per window. Mining of 16 Psyche has to yield enough platinum group metals to justify operations there at further considerable expense. The mass of PGMs returned per window has to be in the kilotonnes. 

### Raise tether top to 6000 km altitude

In this approach, the tether top is simply raised until the release speed at the top is enough to provide an apoapsis at Ceres orbit. The previous skyhook installations all remain the same. The extension is built around them to support the new loads.

- No plane change is provided by release. However, it is possible to get perhaps 4 days of launch window that provides a plane change by the following method:
  
  - Launch when the moon is coming around to cross the Earth's orbit on the retrograde side - somewhat before, and somewhat after, not as it's crossing
  
  - Release the ship when the skyhook is pointed at a small angle to the exact Earth orbit prograde direction, such that the trajectory of the resulting Earth flyby:
    
    - causes a plane change that will have the ship intersect Ceres orbit at the right ecliptic latitude
    
    - bends the flight into the correct prograde direction
  
  - To get the desired plane change, a small burn in the normal direction is needed right after release, but it only needs to be enough to get the ship on course to interact with Earth in the right way

- In cases where Ceres won't be far from the ecliptic at arrival - maybe up to 3° would be ok - this provides enough time to launch enough ships to Ceres for the next one or two return windows on Ceres' end.
  
  - The viability of this depends on how much excess speed can be handled by the spin launcher on the Ceres space elevator, and what range of incoming angles can be accommodated with good confidence of a good capture. 

- During the best windows, where Ceres will be near the ecliptic when the ships arrive, as many ships as possible need to be pushed out, to cover enough following launch windows from Ceres for the launch numbers during sub-optimal windows to be low. 
  
  - There would be perhaps 10 days where the moon's prograde velocity in its solar orbit would be enough, when added with the speed of release, to provide the right apoapsis

- This all means the fleet size has to be large enough for most of it to sit idle most of the time, awaiting launch on either the moon or Ceres. Once each ship has done a number of trips, this is still cheaper than launching ships in a smaller fleet during expensive windows where they'll need to be 60% fuel, or more.

#### Note 1

The energy required to return the momentum lost due to the Ceres ships climbing to the top deck is so great, that optimizations to reduce the needed engine array can really reduce costs. To that end, when launches to Ceres are happening, all other skyhook launch operations cease. The orbit of the whole skyhook is raised by perhaps a few kilometers over a few weeks before the window opens. The foot platform is reeled up by a few km, raising its average altitude. This provides freedom to allow the orbit of the skyhook to decay such that its CoM drops by the amount the orbit was raise, plus the amount the foot platform was raised. (? - how much would it actually decay?) It has to be raised again, but the engines could do that over the course of another few weeks. Stockpiling of mass at low-g points on the skyhook beforehand, and deft movement of mass to manipulate the altitude of the CoM, would allow the backlog of Earth deliveries to be cleared within perhaps a couple of months after the momentum deficit has been cleared. This is manageable since launch windows for Ceres ships occur only once every few years.

#### Note 2

At 85% efficiency for the climber drive trains, they can generate 17.6 GJ of energy per tonne of payload they carry to the top deck, by way of regenerative braking. That is way more than an onboard flywheel could carry unless it was so huge as to be entirely impractical, even in the case of flywheels made of mature CNT fiber. However, as long as the climber can stop often enough to pass that energy to an electrical cable which sends it to the energy storage facilities at the top deck, the onboard flywheel can be an appropriate size. 
In that case, and combined with the strategy of recouping the lost momentum over the course of let's say 50 days, the top deck engines need only produce a continuous 600 MW of jet power per second during that time. That is a much smaller number than the unoptimized case would have required.
